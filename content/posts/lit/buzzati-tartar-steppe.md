A couple of years ago, I turned thirty years old.

It seems to have happened at once over a long meandering arc, but at the same time very quickly. My path was strange. I started as a fail-to-launch "no life," just depression, isolation, and video games (and a lot of time spent on glorious formative corners of cyberspace). Then, relatively quickly, mostly through sheer luck, I came to own a piece of a small company - which gave me enormous flexibility and the means live a relatively responsibility-free life. I got sick of that, for multiple reasons, and made the decision to do something 'serious' and 'adult', because god damn it I shall be an adult, even if I get there late; so I went back to school and got myself an engineering degree. And, after school, I did indeed get what I wanted which was a grown-up adult career. Sometime after that, I met my wife, which was the first serious relationship of my life, and we were married. Within a few months of marriage, we are having our first child.

And, just a couple short weeks before she's born, I read *The Tartar Steppe*, written by Dino Buzzati and first published in 1940.

This bend, just before the arrival of my first child, is the trail marker where I rested for a little bit and read *The Tartar Steppe.*

It is one of these books where "nothing happens." Buzzati's prose is architectural. The story follows a man, Giovanni Drogo (who is often just Drogo), just as he begins his life as a young military officer. He is deployed to a remote fortress in the mountains which overlooks the far boundaryline of his country - a vast stretching desert plain. Drogo is patriotic, idealistic, both proud and trepidatious in his newly minted officer position, tightly clenched in readiness to become the man of stature that he has anticipated he will become. Drogo wants to get into it already. The fortress, which the novel refers to mostly as "The Fort, quickly becomes one of the characters of the story. The mountains impose above it, the endless plain, still and eternal, with soldiers pacing on top of the tall cool walls. 

Drogo's assignment is supposed to be, by the logic of his life, a short one; the remote fortress is far from any action. It's something temporary which of course becomes permanent. The combination of prose, Drogo's martial life, and the eternal setting of the fort create a relentless tick-tock pace, as we are leaed through Drogo's entire life on the remote steppe.

---

There is a concept in Buddhism called ["Taṇhā"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81). The word comes from *"thirst,"* but I've internalized it moreso as *clenching*. Tanha is the emotional, psychological, and often physiological feeling of clenching which happens when one projects his own mind into the future. Of course, one of the central concepts in Buddhism is to release this "future-looking" and bring the mind into the present, into the present moment. To accomplish this, one is supposed to meditate.

This clenching comes from the mechanics of projecting. In order to project your mind into the future, you have to extend out a beam into the future; an arced thing, supported on only on the right-now side. This puts the base of it under tension. Putting more of your mind out into the future is like making the brain at the end of the beam heavier: it subjects its base - the mind and body of the here and now - to a force. Imagine holding onto this beam. The longer it gets, the more you have to strain and exert physical effort and tighten up. And then imagine that you could take some action, to bring the beam in, to make it shorter, to relieve the tension on yourself, until the weight is moved into its natural place, into your center. What would you call that action? 

Lots of people have noted the relationship between anxiety (the habitual practice of forward-looking) with physiological stress. I think, for many people with Tanha, the clenching is mental and physical. The practice of meditation is effective because it works in the body, from the physiology of breathing, to unclench. It is a physical unloading which gathers in the extended beam and brings the brain at its end back to the here and now. 

I have learned in my roughly thirty years of life: first, that I do this, that I have this *Tanha*; second, that it is a physiological thing, a body thing, a literal clenching; and third, that I am capable of gathering up, moving my mind backwards from the forward projection to center it into the present moment, to achieve a stable and confident relief.

The thing that traps Drogo in the Fort in *The Tartar Steppe* is the imagined battle, the glorious singular future moment where the innumerable enemy will come racing across the desert, crashing into the walls of the Fort like a huge wave, with the Fort and Drogo and his men, coiled at the ready to repel the invasion. Drogo's entire forward-looking is drawn into this singularity in the future. It's what keeps him marching back and forth, keeping order; it's his life's purpose. This happens despite the ancient and breathtaking landscape surrounding the Fort. In spite of the beauty of the place. In spite of the cameraderie that the men have there, the connections they make, the actual *life* that Drogo leads, his present moments.

I don't think it would spoil the book to say that the invasion does not happen for Drogo. And this is the genius of the nothing-happens *The Tartar Steppe.*

---

I picked good timing in my personal life to read this book, and I also picked good timing in history.

Buzzati himself was somewhat upper-class, well educated, and a career newspaperman. He wrote for a centrist newspaper, *Corriere della Sera*, throughout the rise and fall of Italian fascism. In the story, Drogo frequently comes into annoying contact with silly orders and intransigent rules, which provide a staccato forward movement of the story. They surely appear because of the ridiculous demands that fascism made on Buzzatti's newspaper position in 1940.

Somewhat more importantly is the structural role of this future-looking in fascism itself. Drogo gives up his entire life for his military career, making a huge personal sacrifice to march back and forth in this remote fort, in service of an imagined future event that never comes. In this way, Buzatti writes sharply about [fascist palingenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palingenetic_ultranationalism) decades before it was articulated by academia and indeed before fascism itself even reached its worst point in Europe.

The fascist instills the belief like this: there is a tremendous, mythological struggle, something like an eschaton, that is about to happen. The nation is going to enter a cataclysmic struggle some time in the near future, and therefore, you (citizen) must make a huge personal sacrifice to prepare for this future struggle. The exact nature of the struggle, the enemy, when it will resolve itself - those are all left un-resolved. The point, whether it is done on purpose or not, is to take the latent Tanha of the populace and redirect it to your own ends. In the novel Drogo and his own Tanha is braided to this mythic civilizational threat, the Tartar invasion. He makes huge sacrifices in service of it. 

These last five or so years have been a real knockout success by my own standards. I have found love, found a career, made my parents proud, and so on. I have internalized the things that I see in Drogo. Their mechanism and action. (though, it is a lifelong battle, of course).

At the same time, there's a lot going on in the world nowadays, and I can't help but see the same Tanha in more and more places, in our social and political lives.  It's very frustrating. Maybe it's why I wrote this essay. 

It is a good time to spend some time to read *The Tartar Steppe*.
